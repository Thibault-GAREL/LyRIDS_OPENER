\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Named entity recognition (NER) in production rarely operates over a fixed inventory of types.
New entity categories appear as a system meets new domains, products or users, and a closed-set tagger trained on a frozen label set cannot name a type it has never seen.
The standard remedy --- re-annotating data for the new types and retraining the model --- is slow and expensive, which is precisely what \emph{open-world} NER seeks to avoid: recognising and typing entities whose categories were not fixed at training time.

Existing open-world approaches buy this flexibility at a cost.
Span-based closed-set models reach high accuracy but remain bound to their training inventory \cite{nguyen2023span}.
OWNER \cite{genest2025owner} removes that bound by training a dedicated entity encoder with a triplet objective and clustering the resulting mentions into dynamically named types, but it still trains an encoder from scratch and pays a heavy per-sentence inference cost.
A complementary line prompts or instruction-tunes billion-parameter language models \cite{zhou2024universalner, sainz2024gollie}, which are accurate but slow, brittle, and often too large for commodity hardware.
Across all of these, evaluation is reported almost exclusively on accuracy: the compute, latency and energy that separate a $137$\,M-parameter pipeline from a $7$\,B-parameter model are left out of the comparison, even though they decide what can actually be deployed.

We present \textbf{OPENER}, a lightweight open-world NER pipeline assembled from off-the-shelf components, with no encoder trained from scratch.
A frozen detector proposes spans, a pretrained Matryoshka sentence embedder --- sharpened by a light contrastive fine-tuning and an error-driven hard-negative mining pass --- maps each span to a vector, and a typing head assigns a type.
The pipeline is modular: detector, embedder and typing head are independently swappable, and the embedder can be truncated to trade quality for compute.
The typing head exposes two operating points.
\textbf{OPENER-Sup}, a supervised linear probe, is the most accurate configuration in our benchmark---the best end-to-end AMI of all compared systems and the best typing on gold mentions---but needs target labels.
A zero-shot variant, \textbf{OPENER-ZS}, uses no target labels at typing time: it types each mention against label-name prototypes, refined transductively and \emph{fused} with the detector's own zero-shot label predictions---a signal that prototype matching otherwise discards.
This zero-shot head is the operating point we put forward: it reaches the best end-to-end AMI of all compared zero-shot systems while keeping an order-of-magnitude-lower energy footprint than the trained-encoder and generative baselines.

Our contributions are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{OPENER}, an open-world NER pipeline assembled entirely from off-the-shelf parts (a frozen detector and a pretrained Matryoshka embedder) with no encoder trained from scratch and a swappable typing head.
\item An \emph{error-driven hard-negative mining} step that fine-tunes the embedding on the type pairs it most often confuses, lifting typing-on-gold AMI by $8.5$ points over plain contrastive fine-tuning.
\item \textbf{OPENER-ZS}, a label-free typing head combining label-name prototypes, a transductive refinement, and a fusion with the detector's zero-shot predictions, which is the best zero-shot system end-to-end on our benchmark, ahead of GLiNER-L, GNER and OWNER.
\item An \emph{energy-aware} benchmark of open-world NER on $13$ datasets that reports quality, latency and energy jointly, separates typing-on-gold from end-to-end, and shows that the dominant open-world bottleneck is mention detection rather than typing, while exposing efficiency gaps that an accuracy-only evaluation would hide. The comparison also includes a small instruction-tuned LLM, Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct in 4-bit---the largest generative model that fits our commodity GPU---which turns out to be both the least accurate and the most expensive of all systems.
\end{itemize}
