Named Entity Recognition (NER) rarely operates over a static, predefined set of entity classes in real-world applications.
Open-world NER therefore asks systems to assign entity types that were not fixed at training time.
Recent systems either train a dedicated encoder or prompt billion-parameter language models, and are compared almost exclusively on accuracy, ignoring compute and energy cost.
We present OPENER, an open-world NER pipeline assembled from ready-to-use components: a frozen mention detector, a Matryoshka-truncatable sentence embedder sharpened by a light contrastive fine-tuning with error-driven hard-negative mining, and a swappable typing head, so that no encoder is trained from scratch.
The typing head comes in two forms.
OPENER-ZS is zero-shot: it types each mention against label-name prototypes and needs no target labels.
OPENER-Sup fits a lightweight supervised probe when a domain's entity classes are fixed and labelled.
We benchmark OPENER against GLiNER, GNER, OWNER and a 4-bit Qwen2.5-1.5B language model on 13 datasets along three axes: quality (Adjusted Mutual Information, AMI), median per-sentence latency, and energy consumption.
Averaged over three embedder-retraining seeds, OPENER-ZS is the most accurate zero-shot system end-to-end ($39.5$ AMI, ahead of GLiNER-L's $38.9$ and OWNER's $34.3$) while staying frugal: $1.7$\,Wh and roughly $180$\,ms per sentence, versus $14.3$\,Wh and $616$\,ms for OWNER.
OPENER-Sup is the most accurate system overall ($40.1$ AMI).
On gold mentions, typing alone reaches $62.3$ AMI, pinning the open-world bottleneck on mention detection.
We release the code, configurations, fine-tuned embedders, and full benchmark.
